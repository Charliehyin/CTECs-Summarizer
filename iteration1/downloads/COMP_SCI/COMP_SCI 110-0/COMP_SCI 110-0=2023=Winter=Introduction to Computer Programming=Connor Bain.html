Take this class as a distro or major requirement, but do not underestimate it. It begins deceptively simple, but quickly gains a thicker layer of complexity as the quarter goes on. I struggled initially because I didn't allocate enough time to work through and completely understand the homework and other assignments, but even with this and taking orgo as a premed, I was able to get an A without too much trouble. My biggest piece of advice is to stay on top of the course flow. Even as an introductory class, it moves quickly and covers a lot of content. Assignments are decently cumulative as well, so don't fall behind. As for a specific learning tactic, I would work through most, if not all, of the practice problems the prof. introduces in the lectures. This is especially helpful for the quizzes, which are very representative of what you learn in class. So make sure you learn.

The class is broken down as follows: projects (x2), homework (x6, can drop 1), quizzes (x3, can drop 1), in class "tutorials" (x7, can drop 1), and in class "mini–quizzes" (x6, canNOT drop any). Tutorials are just shorter homework assignments that you have the opportunity to do in class or remotely, and mini–quizzes are simply graded–for–participation in–class quizzes. As you can see, there's a lot, but it's only overwhelming if you fall behind on learning the content.


As for the teacher, prof. Bain is great. He's very upbeat and passionate to teach, and always willing to give effective advice – but not the hand–held kind that explains everything to you. We use a platform called Campuswire to ask him and the TAs questions (just Piazza with a better UI) and he's usually pretty quick to reply.

In all, I enjoyed this course and learned a lot. The best thing was that I came out of it with a sense of pride for my work (code I wrote for assignments), which is hard to find in STEM classes.

Buckle up, friends.

Pros:
– Attendance is rewarded – if you attend Tutorials and Mini–Quiz days, you get free 100s, and they really make a difference because combined they are worth 20% of your grade.
– Good class to take with friends – I would not have done it alone, truthfully.
– The first four or five weeks are very digestible. If you, like me, had no experience with coding before this course, your attention to the lectures and material will allow you to do well. That is, for the first four or five weeks...
– Homework assignments are due on Mondays before class, and you get about a week to do them. Definitely ample time.
– If you find a good PM, they will save your life and be so helpful and thoroughly explain the content so you know how to apply it. Some PMs and TAs are amazing.

Cons:
–CONnor
– Things go haywire after the first four weeks, and you will notice the shift in lectures, assignments, etc. This is partially because of the quarter system's structure, but lectures start to feel like a coloring book: you're given the outline, but not only do you have to buy colored pencils, but you have to fill the lines in on your own. Mind you, it is absolutely NOT a color–by–number book.
– If you have no experience with coding, this will NOT be an Easy A. You HAVE to go to Office Hours––much emphasis on the plural. You have to go to multiple sessions multiple times a day. This is a HUGE time commitment!!!!!! The only reason I finished with an A is because I took 3 classes this quarter. I can't imagine taking this along with 3+ other classes. I am a non–major, and this class was designed for non–majors, so I think it's fair for me to be frustrated with this.
– Office Hours can be useless. The PMs do not have a great approach to helping students. If you want the help you need from most of them, you must be one of the loudest in the room or grab them by the arm. I'm not joking, either. I've seen multiple people do this and be helped ahead of those waiting for forty minutes. It's happened to me, too. I sat there with my hand raised for an entire Office Hour session and was told twice by the PM: "I'll help you next." Then, of course, someone else would practically pull the PM into their lap to look at their code and take time away from the rest of us. All in all, if you have a good PM, they will tell the others to wait and go in order.
– Connor might be a nice guy, but he can be pretty passive–aggressive at times, and it's hard for us who have no Computer Science experience and are essentially learning a new language.
– Mini–quizzes and lectures are NOTHING like the homework or quizzes, hence why although I never missed a class I had to be in Office Hours multiple times a day and study day in and day out. The homework and quizzes are Kool–Aid, and the mini–quizzes and lectures are water––not even watered–down Kool–Aid––they are WATER with, if you're lucky, a hint of fruit punch. The examples in class need to be more complex because that's what the homework is like.
– PMs cancel their Office Hours CONSTANTLY. I understand they have other responsibilities and things come up, but they signed up for this too. Cancellations happen too frequently and you are left to your own devices, especially when they don't cancel until the night before you planned on going to get help. Sometimes, they don't even update the Google Sheet and you, with a bunch of other kids who have no idea what to do, sit in a PM–less room for an hour, and you leave with the same TypeError you had when you walked in.

Overall rating of the class: 3.5/10
